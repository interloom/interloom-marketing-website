Software may have eaten parts of the world, but in the industries that keep it running — insurance, logistics, real estate management, construction, manufacturing — digitalisation remains superficial. The raw capability of AI is soaring, yet it's almost nowhere to be found in production workflows.

The core issue is a lack of unified context. Over 70 percent of knowledge work happens in departmental silos, scattered across tools, documents, emails, and meetings. Tasks get done, but knowledge is lost — undocumented, unstructured, impossible to re-use. In a world with abundant artificial intelligence it's a critical point of failure.

## The capability gap nobody talks about

Open any AI demo and you'll see something impressive. Summarisation, classification, extraction, reasoning — all of it works beautifully on stage. Then someone tries to apply it to a real underwriting workflow or a freight exception process, and the whole thing stalls.

Not because the model isn't capable. Because the model has no idea what "normal" looks like for that team, that client, that product line. It has no access to the thirty-page email thread from last quarter that established precedent. It can't read the senior handler's mental model of which cases need escalation and which don't.

The intelligence is there. The context isn't.

## Why the tooling gap persists

The industries most in need of automation are the ones least well served by existing tools. Enterprise workflow platforms assume you have a team of developers to build and maintain integrations. Low-code tools work for simple sequences but collapse under the weight of real operational complexity — branching logic, exceptions, human judgement calls, regulatory constraints.

So teams default to what works: spreadsheets, email, and the institutional memory of whoever has been around the longest. It's inefficient, fragile, and completely undocumented, but it ships.

The result is a paradox. The organisations that would benefit most from AI are the ones whose knowledge is hardest for AI to reach.

## Context is not data

There's a common misconception that the fix is a data project. Clean the data, build a warehouse, connect an LLM, and the intelligence follows. But operational knowledge isn't data in the traditional sense. It's the reasoning behind decisions. It's the exception patterns that took years to learn. It's the difference between what the process document says and what actually happens on the floor.

Data tells you what occurred. Context tells you why, and what to do next time.

Most organisations have plenty of data. What they lack is structured, accessible context — the kind that a new team member or an AI agent can draw on at the point of decision.

## What changes when context is unified

When operational knowledge is captured, structured, and made available to every workflow at execution time, several things shift simultaneously.

First, AI becomes useful in production. A language model grounded in six months of accumulated case decisions doesn't hallucinate policy — it references precedent. It doesn't guess at process — it follows the logic that experienced people have refined case by case.

Second, expertise scales. The gap between a ten-year veteran and a new hire shrinks, not because the veteran is replaced but because their accumulated judgement is now part of the system. Every correction, every override, every edge case handled feeds back into a knowledge layer that makes the next decision better.

Third, operations become auditable by default. When context flows through a structured layer rather than through informal channels, every decision has a traceable rationale. Compliance stops being a retrospective exercise and becomes a natural output of doing the work.

## The compounding advantage

None of this happens overnight. A context layer is built incrementally — case by case, correction by correction. But it compounds in a way that traditional automation does not.

A rules engine is as good on day one thousand as it is on day one. A context-driven system is measurably better, because it has absorbed a thousand days of decisions, exceptions, and refinements. That accumulated understanding is proprietary, portable, and impossible to replicate by buying better technology.

The organisations that start capturing context now will have an advantage that widens with every case processed. The ones that wait will find themselves trying to bridge an ever-growing gap with ever-more-powerful models that still don't understand their business.

Intelligence is abundant. Context is scarce. The bottleneck has moved, and the companies that recognise it first will move with it.
